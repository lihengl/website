2. Methods:

Below is a list of what I modifications I made to the original motion data in order to achieve each different walk styles.

  • Robotic:
    1. Rotate forarms to align with upperarms
    2. Scale down oscillation angles of the forarms to almost zero
    3. Scale up z(up) translation of the feet IKs
    4. Rotate lowerback backwards a bit
    5. Rotate neck backwards a bit

  • Determined:
    1. Rotate forwarms forward a bit
    2. Translate feet IKs backward a bit
    3. Rotate lowerback forward a bit (both 2. and 3. were to lean the character forward)
    4. Rotate neck backward a bit to make the character still looking forward

  • Relaxed:
    1. Rotate lowerback backward
    2. Scale up the oscillation angles of both arms

  • Defeated:
    1. Rotate lowerback forward for a fair amount of degree
    2. Scale down z(up) translation of feet IKs

  • Fearless:
    1. Move x(forward) translation forward so that the feet goes a bit futher than the whole body
    2. Rotate backarms backward a bit
    3. Rotate lowerback backward a bit
    4. Rotate upperback forward a bit
    5. Scale down the oscillation angles of both arms

  • Not-So-Manly:
    1. Rotate arms to the shape as seen in the video (could not explain this better...)
    2. Rotate legs toward the center of the body
    3. Scale down z(up) translation of the feet IKs
    4. Scale up the oscillation angles of lowerback
    5. Scale up the oscillation angles of neck about x(forward) axis

  • Sneaky:
    1. Move z(up) translation of root downward with a fair amount of distance
    2. Rotate lowerback forward with a fair amount of degree
    3. Rotate neck backward until the character is looking forward again
    4. Rotate forearms to align with backarms
    5. Rotate backarms away from the center of the body
    6. Scale up the oscillation angles of lowerback
    7. Scale down the oscillation angles of backarms
    8. Scale down z(up) translation of the feet IKs

  • Joyful:
    1. Scale up the oscillation distance of z(up) translation of root
    2. Scale up z(up) translation distance of the feet IKs
    3. Rotate lowerback backward a bit
    4. Rotate neck backward for a fair amount of degree
    5. Rotate forearms until they are in right angles with backarms
    6. Scale up the oscillation angles of backarms with a fair amount of degree

3. Observations:

After playing around with the Maya software which I almost had no prior experience with, I figured out that for the purpose of editing, not starting a new animation from scratch, using the Graph Editor in Maya is a good way to go. I have only used the Graph Editor for this assignment. There are probably a lot more tools that Maya has to provide, but given the time contraint, it is the tool of my choice. Several observations I found is listed below:

  • It is really easy to break the animation by modifying timing.
    Every attributes of the bones in the skeleton, including translations, rotations, and scale are all values over time. As I was trying to see what moving around the graph will do, I discovered that whenever I tried to modify the timing, it is very easy to make the animation "look wrong". For example, one thing I was trying to do is to make the character walk more "powerful", and I was hoping to achieve it by having both the feet and the arms reach their local destination (or, local peak value on the graph) faster. The way I tried was to steepen the slope of the sin-like graph of the rotation attibutes while maintain their period. However, the result by simply doing is looked weird, and I belive it has to do with that not only the arm and the feet, but also the torso the root, or basically the whole body needs to adjust to that speed change in order for the motion to look natural or believable. If time permit I might be able to conquer this, but there was no easy way to do it effectively during my experiment.

  • The spine bones matter a lot
    The very first effective change that I made was the overall angle of the lowerback bone. The angle of lowerback bone determines the leaning angle of the body, and this determines a large part of the style of our character. Generally leaning forward makes the character looks more focused, while leaning backward makes the character looks more relaxed or absent- minded. However, if leaning forward to a large amount of degree, it will turn into quite a nother result as the "defeated" one shown in the video.

  • Oscillation angles also matters a lot
    Walking is a cycling motion. Except for the x(forward)-translation in this assignment, which moves the character forward, everything is osillating in some waveform if viewed from the Graph Editor. For almost every motions the amplitude of the waveform is something I would definitely play around with. Not only they changes the style/feel of the motions a lot, but also I found them relatively safe to play around with. That means, as long as you do not modify them too far, the resulting animtation would remain in a decent quality state.
